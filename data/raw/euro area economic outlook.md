# Euro Area Economic Outlook: Inflation Dynamics and Monetary Policy Transmission

## Abstract

This report provides an assessment of inflation developments and monetary policy transmission mechanisms in the euro area between 2022 and early 2026, based on recent data and institutional projections. The analysis focuses on the persistence of core inflation, the lagged effects of monetary tightening, and the implications for economic growth and financial stability, drawing on evidence from central banks and international financial institutions.

## 1. Introduction

The euro area economy underwent significant macroeconomic adjustments between 2022 and 2025 following a sequence of global shocks, including the post-pandemic recovery, energy market disruptions, and geopolitical tensions.

Headline inflation surged to approximately 10.6% year-on-year in October 2022, driven primarily by energy and food price increases. This marked the highest inflation level since the introduction of the euro. In response, the European Central Bank implemented a rapid tightening cycle aimed at restoring price stability.

By 2024 and 2025, inflation began to moderate, reflecting both base effects and the gradual transmission of higher interest rates into the real economy. However, underlying inflationary pressures—particularly in the services sector—remained persistent.

## 2. Macroeconomic Developments

### 2.1 Inflation Trends

The disinflation process has been uneven across components:

- Headline inflation declined from double-digit levels in 2022 to approximately 2.4% by the end of 2025  
- Core inflation (excluding energy and food) remained elevated, averaging around 3.0–3.2% in 2025  
- Services inflation remained above 3.5%, reflecting continued wage pressures  

#### Key drivers

**Energy Price Dynamics**  
Energy prices declined significantly from their 2022 peaks. European gas prices (TTF) fell from levels above €300/MWh in 2022 to below €50/MWh in 2024, contributing to the disinflation process.

**Supply Chain Normalization**  
Global supply bottlenecks eased throughout 2023, reducing input costs in manufacturing and supporting a decline in goods inflation.

**Wage Growth and Second-Round Effects**  
Nominal wages increased by approximately 4.5–5.0% in 2025, reflecting tight labor market conditions and delayed pass-through of past inflation.

**Inflation Expectations**  
Survey-based and market-based measures indicate that medium-term inflation expectations stabilized around 2.0–2.2%, consistent with the ECB’s target.

### 2.2 Economic Growth

Economic activity slowed considerably following the tightening cycle:

| Year | Real GDP Growth (%) |
|------|--------------------|
| 2022 | 3.4                |
| 2023 | 0.5                |
| 2024 | 0.8                |
| 2025 | 1.1–1.3 (est.)     |
| 2026 | 1.3–1.5 (proj.)    |

#### Growth drivers and constraints

Private consumption remained supported by employment growth and excess savings accumulated during the pandemic, although it was constrained by declining real incomes in 2022–2023. A gradual recovery in purchasing power is supporting consumption in 2025–2026.

Investment weakened, particularly in interest-sensitive sectors such as construction and real estate, as higher financing costs and uncertainty weighed on business decisions.

Net exports provided a limited contribution due to weak global demand, particularly from major trading partners.

High-frequency indicators point to a gradual recovery, with notable heterogeneity across euro area economies.

### 2.3 Labor Market

The euro area labor market has remained resilient:

- Unemployment rate: approximately 6.4–6.6% in 2025  
- Participation rates have increased, including among older workers  
- Vacancy rates remain elevated, particularly in services  

#### Structural observations

Labor shortages persist in sectors such as healthcare, construction, and hospitality. Wage-setting mechanisms have become more responsive to inflation developments, contributing to stronger nominal wage growth.

Real wages are gradually recovering but remain below pre-inflation levels in several economies. Tight labor market conditions continue to exert upward pressure on core inflation.

## 3. Monetary Policy and Financial Conditions

### 3.1 Policy Tightening Cycle

Between July 2022 and September 2023, the ECB increased policy rates from 0% to approximately 4.0%, representing one of the fastest tightening episodes in the history of the euro area.

Key policy objectives included anchoring inflation expectations, reducing excess demand, and preventing second-round inflation effects.

### 3.2 Transmission Mechanisms

#### Interest Rate Channel

Higher policy rates translated into increased borrowing costs across the economy:

- Mortgage rates rose from around 1.5% in 2021 to above 4% in 2024  
- Corporate lending rates increased significantly, particularly for small and medium-sized enterprises  

#### Credit Channel

Bank lending standards tightened considerably, as reflected in ECB Bank Lending Survey data:

- Loan growth declined from approximately 6% in 2022 to near zero in 2024  
- Credit demand weakened across both households and firms  

#### Asset Price Channel

Housing markets adjusted to higher interest rates, with price stagnation or declines observed in several countries and a reduction in transaction volumes.

Equity markets experienced increased volatility and valuation adjustments due to higher discount rates.

#### Expectations Channel

Forward guidance and policy credibility contributed to a gradual re-anchoring of inflation expectations toward the medium-term target.

### 3.3 Financial Conditions

By 2025:

- Real interest rates turned positive  
- Financing conditions remained restrictive  
- Debt servicing costs increased for households, firms, and governments  

Despite tighter conditions, financial fragmentation within the euro area remained limited.

## 4. Outlook and Risks

### 4.1 Baseline Scenario

- Inflation projected to converge toward approximately 2.0–2.2% by 2026  
- Core inflation gradually declining toward target  
- GDP growth expected to reach around 1.3–1.5% in 2026  
- Gradual easing of monetary policy anticipated, conditional on incoming data  

### 4.2 Upside Risks to Inflation

- Stronger-than-expected wage growth  
- Renewed increases in energy prices  
- More expansionary fiscal policies in large economies  

### 4.3 Downside Risks to Growth

- Persistent weakness in external demand  
- Tighter-than-expected credit conditions  
- Financial stress in highly leveraged corporate sectors  

### 4.4 Structural Risks

- Geopolitical fragmentation affecting trade flows  
- Costs associated with the energy transition  
- Demographic pressures on labor supply and potential growth
