# Spain: Macroeconomic Performance and Structural Outlook

## Abstract

Between 2022 and 2025, the Spanish economy displayed a comparatively strong performance within the euro area, supported by robust services activity, tourism inflows, and the deployment of European recovery funds. Growth remained above the euro area average during most of this period, although momentum gradually moderated as monetary policy tightened and external demand weakened.

Inflation peaked in 2022 at levels close to 11%, driven primarily by energy and food shocks, before declining steadily through 2023 and 2024. Labour market conditions improved significantly compared to pre-pandemic levels, although structural unemployment remained elevated relative to core euro area economies.

## 1. Introduction

Spain entered the post-pandemic period with strong cyclical momentum, benefiting from the rebound in domestic consumption, a rapid recovery in tourism, and supportive fiscal and European-level policy measures. Real GDP surpassed pre-pandemic levels in 2022, earlier than several large euro area peers.

However, from 2023 onwards, growth dynamics became increasingly influenced by tighter financial conditions, higher interest rates, and weaker external demand from key European trading partners such as Germany and France. At the same time, the gradual withdrawal of pandemic-related fiscal support contributed to a moderation in domestic demand.

## 2. Economic Growth and Demand Composition

Spain’s real GDP growth remained relatively strong in comparative terms, although it decelerated progressively from the post-pandemic rebound phase.

Real GDP growth is estimated at approximately 5.8% in 2022, followed by 2.5% in 2023, around 2.0% in 2024, and close to 1.7% in 2025. This profile reflects a normalization from exceptionally high post-pandemic growth rates toward more sustainable medium-term levels.

Private consumption was a central driver of activity throughout the period. Employment growth and nominal wage increases supported household income, although real purchasing power was temporarily eroded during the inflation peak in 2022–2023. As inflation moderated, real disposable income began to recover, supporting a gradual improvement in consumption conditions.

Investment dynamics were more mixed. Public investment benefited from the implementation of the NextGenerationEU framework, which supported infrastructure, digitalization, and green transition projects. However, private investment slowed notably as interest rates increased and financing conditions tightened. Construction activity, in particular, showed signs of weakness due to higher mortgage rates and reduced housing affordability.

The external sector continued to play a significant role in Spain’s growth performance. Tourism recovered strongly, exceeding pre-pandemic levels in several periods and contributing positively to services exports. At the same time, goods exports faced headwinds from weaker global manufacturing demand. The trade balance improved in 2023–2024, supported by lower energy import costs compared to the 2022 peak.

## 3. Inflation Dynamics

Inflation in Spain followed a similar trajectory to the broader euro area, although with some notable differences in timing and magnitude.

Headline inflation peaked at approximately 10.8% in 2022, driven primarily by energy and food prices. By 2024, inflation had declined to around 3.5% on average, and further moderation was observed in 2025, with estimates close to 2.6%. Core inflation remained more persistent, averaging around 3.2% in 2025, reflecting services sector dynamics and wage adjustments.

Energy inflation declined more rapidly in Spain than in several other euro area economies. This was partly due to policy interventions, including regulated pricing mechanisms and temporary fiscal measures aimed at limiting household exposure to electricity and gas price volatility. The Iberian exception in energy pricing dynamics contributed to relatively lower inflation volatility.

Food inflation remained elevated during 2023, reflecting global commodity price pressures and domestic supply chain effects. Services inflation, however, emerged as the most persistent component, driven by strong demand in tourism-related activities and gradual wage increases across labour-intensive sectors.

Temporary fiscal measures, including VAT reductions on selected food items and energy subsidies, helped mitigate short-term inflation pressures. However, these measures had implications for public finances and were gradually phased out as inflation moderated.

## 4. Labour Market Developments

The Spanish labour market improved significantly over the period, both in cyclical and structural terms.

The unemployment rate declined from approximately 13% in 2021 to around 11.5% in 2025. Although this level remains high compared to the euro area average, it represents a notable improvement relative to historical standards in Spain.

Employment growth was concentrated in services sectors, particularly tourism, hospitality, and retail. These sectors benefited directly from the post-pandemic recovery in mobility and international travel. Industrial employment showed more modest gains, reflecting weaker external demand conditions.

A key structural development was the impact of the 2021 labour market reform, which contributed to a reduction in temporary employment and an increase in permanent contracts. This shift improved job stability and reduced employment volatility, particularly in sectors historically characterized by high labour turnover.

Wage dynamics reflected the inflation environment. Nominal wages increased at rates of approximately 3.5–4.0% annually during 2023–2025. However, real wages declined initially during the inflation surge and only began to recover as inflation moderated. Importantly, while wage pressures contributed to services inflation, there was no clear evidence of a self-reinforcing wage-price spiral at the aggregate level.

## 5. Fiscal Policy and Public Finances

Spain’s fiscal position improved gradually over the period, although public debt remained elevated.

The fiscal deficit declined from approximately 4.8% of GDP in 2022 to around 3.2% in 2025. This consolidation was driven by the withdrawal of emergency energy support measures, improved tax revenues linked to nominal growth, and the gradual normalization of economic activity.

Public debt remained broadly stable at around 108% of GDP in 2025. While high, debt dynamics were supported by relatively strong nominal GDP growth, which helped stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio despite higher interest costs. However, the increase in interest rates led to a gradual rise in debt servicing costs, placing additional pressure on fiscal space.

Fiscal risks remain relevant. These include long-term pressures associated with population ageing, potential contingent liabilities from past support measures, and continued reliance on European funding mechanisms for investment programs.

## 6. Financial Sector Conditions

The Spanish banking sector remained resilient throughout the period, supported by improved profitability and strong capital positions.

Non-performing loan ratios declined to below 3% by 2025, reflecting improved asset quality and favourable macroeconomic conditions relative to previous crisis episodes. Rising interest rates improved net interest margins, contributing to stronger profitability for banks.

However, risks have increased in certain segments. Exposure to the real estate sector remains a structural vulnerability, particularly in the context of higher mortgage rates and slower housing market activity. Household borrowing costs increased significantly, leading to a moderation in credit demand. While the banking system has remained stable, credit standards tightened in line with broader euro area trends.

## 7. Outlook and Risks

The baseline outlook for Spain points to moderate but stable growth, with GDP expected to expand by approximately 1.8% in 2026. Inflation is projected to stabilize close to 2.2%, consistent with broader euro area convergence toward the ECB target. Real incomes are expected to continue recovering gradually as inflation stabilizes and wage growth remains positive.

Downside risks include weaker external demand from key European partners, particularly if industrial activity in the euro area remains subdued. A prolonged period of high interest rates could also weigh on investment and housing markets. In addition, fiscal constraints may limit policy flexibility in the event of new shocks.

Upside risks are primarily associated with stronger-than-expected tourism performance, more efficient deployment of EU recovery funds, and productivity gains linked to digitalization and green investment. Structural reforms that improve labour market efficiency could also enhance medium-term growth potential.

## 8. Structural Challenges and Policy Priorities

Despite recent improvements, Spain continues to face several structural challenges that constrain long-term growth potential. Structural unemployment remains elevated, particularly among younger cohorts and in certain regions. Productivity growth has been relatively weak compared to leading euro area economies, reflecting both sectoral composition and limited innovation intensity in parts of the economy.

Regional disparities remain significant, with persistent differences in income levels and employment rates across autonomous communities. Demographic ageing is also expected to exert increasing pressure on labour supply and public finances over the medium to long term.

Policy priorities therefore remain focused on improving labour market efficiency, enhancing education and skills alignment with labour demand, accelerating digital transformation, and strengthening fiscal sustainability. Continued investment in green transition initiatives is also expected to play a central role in supporting long-term competitiveness.

## 9. Conclusion

Spain has demonstrated notable resilience in the face of multiple overlapping shocks, including the pandemic recovery, the energy crisis, and the tightening of global financial conditions. Strong performance in the services sector and tourism has supported growth above the euro area average, while labour market reforms have contributed to improved employment stability.

However, medium-term prospects remain dependent on addressing structural weaknesses, particularly in productivity, labour market segmentation, and regional disparities. Maintaining fiscal discipline while supporting investment in structural transformation will be critical for sustaining convergence within the euro area over the coming years.
