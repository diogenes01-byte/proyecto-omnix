# Global Market Analysis: Interest Rates, Equity Markets, and Financial Conditions

## Executive Summary

Between 2022 and 2025, global financial markets underwent a broad and rapid repricing driven by the most aggressive monetary tightening cycle since the early 1980s. Policy rates in advanced economies increased by 400–525 basis points within a relatively short period, pushing sovereign yields to multi-year highs and significantly tightening global financial conditions.

Government bond yields rose sharply, equity markets experienced pronounced volatility with periods of correction and recovery, and credit conditions deteriorated across both advanced and emerging economies. Despite these pressures, financial systems remained broadly resilient, supported by strong bank capitalization and post-2008 regulatory frameworks.

## 1. Macro-Financial Background

The macro-financial environment during this period was shaped by three interrelated dynamics: restrictive monetary policy, a gradual but incomplete disinflation process, and a marked slowdown in global economic growth.

Central banks, including the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of England, raised policy rates from near-zero levels in 2021 to restrictive territory by 2023–2024. In the United States, the federal funds rate reached 5.25–5.50%, while the ECB deposit facility rate peaked at 4.00%. This tightening occurred alongside balance sheet reduction, further reinforcing restrictive conditions.

Inflation declined from its 2022 peaks—above 9% in the United States and over 10% in the euro area—but remained above target through most of 2024. At the same time, global growth slowed from approximately 6.0% in 2021 to around 3.0–3.2% between 2023 and 2025, according to IMF estimates.

The combination of high interest rates, moderating inflation, and weaker growth led to a synchronized tightening in financial conditions across global markets.

## 2. Fixed Income Markets

### 2.1 Government Bond Yields

Sovereign bond markets experienced a significant repricing as investors adjusted to a higher-for-longer interest rate environment.

In the United States, 10-year Treasury yields rose from approximately 1.5% in early 2021 to peaks between 4.5% and 5.0% during 2023–2024. In the euro area, German Bund yields increased from negative levels (-0.5% in 2021) to around 2.5–3.0%. Similar dynamics were observed in the United Kingdom, where 10-year gilt yields exceeded 4.0% at various points.

This adjustment reflected a combination of factors, including higher expected policy rates, persistent inflation risk premia, and the withdrawal of central bank demand due to quantitative tightening.

### 2.2 Yield Curve Dynamics

A defining feature of this period was the inversion of yield curves across major economies. Short-term rates rose above long-term yields, with the US 2-year/10-year spread turning negative by more than 100 basis points at its deepest inversion in 2023.

Historically, such inversions have been reliable indicators of future economic slowdowns. The persistence of inverted yield curves into 2024 and parts of 2025 reflected market expectations that restrictive monetary policy would eventually lead to weaker growth and subsequent rate cuts.

### 2.3 Corporate Bond Markets

Corporate credit markets tightened significantly as borrowing costs increased and investor risk appetite declined.

Investment-grade credit spreads widened moderately, typically fluctuating between 120 and 180 basis points over government benchmarks. In contrast, high-yield spreads expanded more sharply, often exceeding 400–500 basis points during periods of stress.

Issuance volumes declined notably during peak tightening phases in 2022 and early 2023, particularly in lower-rated segments. Firms with weaker balance sheets faced higher refinancing risks, while stronger issuers maintained market access at elevated costs.

Overall, the environment led to a clear differentiation across credit quality, with investors demanding higher compensation for risk.

## 3. Equity Markets

### 3.1 Market Performance

Global equity markets experienced significant volatility over the period. In 2022, major indices declined sharply as interest rates rose and valuation multiples compressed. The S&P 500 fell by approximately 20% during the year, while European indices recorded comparable declines.

In 2023 and 2024, equity markets partially recovered, supported by resilient corporate earnings and improving sentiment around disinflation. However, performance remained uneven across regions and sectors.

Technology and growth stocks were particularly sensitive to changes in interest rates, given their reliance on long-duration cash flows. As discount rates increased, valuations in these sectors adjusted more sharply.

### 3.2 Valuation Adjustments

Higher interest rates led to a repricing of equity valuations. Price-to-earnings (P/E) ratios declined from elevated levels observed during the low-rate environment of 2020–2021. In the United States, forward P/E ratios fell from above 21x to closer to 17–18x during the tightening phase.

The equity risk premium increased as investors demanded greater compensation relative to rising bond yields. At the same time, earnings expectations were revised downward in several sectors, reflecting slower economic growth.

Despite these adjustments, strong earnings in sectors such as technology and energy helped stabilize overall market performance.

### 3.3 Sectoral Trends

Sectoral divergence was a defining feature of equity markets during this period. Energy companies benefited from elevated commodity prices, particularly in 2022, while financial institutions saw improved profitability due to higher net interest margins.

In contrast, real estate sectors experienced significant pressure due to rising financing costs and declining property valuations. Consumer discretionary sectors were also affected by reduced purchasing power and tighter credit conditions.

## 4. Financial Conditions

Composite financial conditions indices from institutions such as the Federal Reserve and the ECB indicate a substantial tightening between 2022 and 2024. This tightening was driven by higher interest rates, wider credit spreads, lower equity valuations, and stronger exchange rates in key currencies.

By 2025, financial conditions showed signs of stabilization, although they remained restrictive compared to pre-2022 levels. This environment contributed to slower credit growth, reduced investment, and weaker aggregate demand.

## 5. Cross-Asset Interactions

The interaction between asset classes became more pronounced during the tightening cycle. Rising interest rates reduced the present value of future earnings, leading to a stronger negative correlation between bond yields and equity prices.

Credit markets acted as an early signal of economic stress, with widening spreads often preceding equity market corrections. At the same time, currency markets played a critical role in transmitting global financial conditions.

The US dollar appreciated significantly during 2022, reaching parity with the euro for the first time in two decades. This appreciation tightened financial conditions globally, particularly in emerging markets, by increasing the cost of servicing dollar-denominated debt.

## 6. Risks and Forward-Looking Scenarios

The global financial outlook remains subject to several key risks. A prolonged period of high interest rates could lead to further tightening in financial conditions, increasing the probability of asset price corrections and liquidity stress in certain market segments.

On the upside, a faster-than-expected decline in inflation could allow central banks to begin easing policy earlier, supporting both bond and equity markets. Resilient corporate earnings, particularly in advanced economies, could also contribute to improved market sentiment.

Tail risks include financial instability in highly leveraged sectors, sovereign debt stress in vulnerable economies, and renewed geopolitical shocks affecting energy and commodity markets.

## 7. Outlook

Looking ahead, financial markets are expected to remain highly sensitive to macroeconomic data releases and central bank communication. Inflation developments, labor market conditions, and policy signals will continue to shape market expectations.

Baseline scenarios suggest a gradual normalization of yield curves as monetary policy shifts toward easing, alongside a moderate recovery in equity valuations and stabilization in credit spreads. However, uncertainty remains elevated, and market adjustments are likely to remain data-dependent.
