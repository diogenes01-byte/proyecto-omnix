# Inflation Report: Euro Area Price Dynamics and Core Inflation Persistence (2022–2025)

## Abstract

This report analyses inflation dynamics in advanced economies, with a primary focus on the euro area between 2022 and 2025. The inflationary episode is decomposed into supply-driven shocks, demand-side pressures, and structural persistence in services inflation. The analysis draws on datasets and publications from the European Central Bank (ECB), Eurostat, and the International Monetary Fund (IMF).

Results indicate that while headline inflation has declined significantly from its 2022 peak, underlying core inflation remains more persistent due to wage adjustments, lagged monetary transmission, and sectoral rigidity in services.

## 1. Overview of Inflation Developments (2022–2025)

Inflation in the euro area reached multi-decade highs in 2022, exceeding 10% at its peak, driven primarily by energy and food price shocks. By 2024–2025, inflation moderated significantly but remained above the 2% target threshold for extended periods.

Key patterns observed include a sharp peak in 2022 due to the energy crisis, followed by gradual disinflation during 2023–2025. A persistent divergence between headline and core inflation is also evident, alongside heterogeneous inflation dynamics across member states.

## 2. Components of Inflation

### 2.1 Headline Inflation

Headline inflation reflects total consumer price changes, heavily influenced by volatile components such as energy, food commodities, and transport costs. During 2022, energy alone accounted for more than half of total inflation in several euro area economies.

### 2.2 Core Inflation

Core inflation excludes energy and food and provides a clearer signal of underlying price pressures. Persistent drivers include services inflation, wage growth dynamics, and indexation mechanisms in contracts. Even after energy price normalization, core inflation remained elevated, indicating structural stickiness.

## 3. Supply-Side Drivers

The inflation surge was initially driven by supply-side shocks. A major factor was the reduction in Russian gas supply to Europe, combined with volatility in global oil markets and increased dependency on LNG imports. In parallel, post-COVID logistics bottlenecks and semiconductor shortages disrupted manufacturing, while shipping costs experienced significant volatility between 2021 and 2022.

Food prices were also affected by higher fertilizer costs, agricultural disruptions, and climate-related shocks in key producing regions.

## 4. Demand-Side Pressures

Although supply shocks dominated early inflation, demand factors reinforced persistence. These included excess savings accumulated during the pandemic, fiscal stimulus measures implemented in 2020–2021, strong labour market conditions, and resilient consumption in services sectors.

## 5. Wage Dynamics and Inflation Persistence

Wage growth became a key transmission mechanism in 2023–2025. Nominal wages increased with a lag relative to inflation, while partial indexation in collective bargaining agreements reinforced adjustment dynamics. Tight labour markets in services sectors contributed to persistent wage pressures, particularly in hospitality, retail, and public services.

## 6. Monetary Policy Impact on Inflation

Monetary tightening by the European Central Bank significantly influenced inflation trajectories through multiple channels. Higher policy rates reduced credit demand and investment, while a stronger euro reduced imported inflation. Tighter lending standards constrained household consumption and corporate expansion. Forward guidance also contributed to re-anchoring long-term inflation expectations closer to target levels.

## 7. Cross-Country Heterogeneity

Inflation dynamics varied across the euro area:

- Germany: strong exposure to energy shocks and industrial sensitivity  
- Spain: faster disinflation supported by energy mix and tourism recovery  
- Italy: higher persistence in services inflation  
- France: regulated energy pricing dampened volatility  

## 8. Inflation Decomposition (Stylised)

Approximate contributions during the peak inflation period:

- Energy: 40–60% of headline inflation  
- Food: 15–25%  
- Services: 20–30%  
- Non-energy industrial goods: 5–10%  

## 9. Policy Implications

The episode highlights the importance of energy diversification strategies in addressing supply shocks, as well as the limitations of monetary policy in tackling supply-driven inflation. Structural reforms are necessary to reduce services inflation rigidity, while wage coordination mechanisms can influence persistence dynamics.


The 2022–2025 inflation cycle reflects a complex interaction between external shocks, domestic demand resilience, and structural labour market dynamics. While monetary tightening has been effective in reducing headline inflation, core inflation remains more persistent, underscoring the importance of structural factors in achieving durable price stability.
