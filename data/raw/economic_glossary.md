# Economic Glossary

## Terms

### Inflation
**Definition:** Sustained and generalized increase in the price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time.  
**Source:** Bank of Spain / IMF  
**Example:** If inflation is 3% per year, a €100 good costs €103 the following year.

### Deflation
**Definition:** Sustained decrease in the general price level in an economy.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** If prices fall by 2% per year, a €100 product costs €98.

### GDP
**Definition:** Total value of final goods and services produced in a country during a given period.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** GDP measures the size of a country's economy.

### Unemployment
**Definition:** Situation in which people who want to work cannot find employment.  
**Source:** INE  
**Example:** An unemployment rate of 10% implies that 10 out of every 100 active people do not have a job.

### Interest Rate
**Definition:** Price of money over time, expressed as a percentage.  
**Source:** ECB  
**Example:** A loan with 5% annual interest generates €5 for every €100 lent.

### Supply
**Definition:** Quantity of goods or services that producers are willing to sell at different prices.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** If the price rises, producers usually offer more units.

### Demand
**Definition:** Quantity of goods or services that consumers are willing to buy at different prices.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** If the price falls, consumers tend to buy more.

### Elasticity
**Definition:** Measure of the sensitivity of demand or supply to changes in price.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** A product with high elasticity changes its demand significantly with price variations.

### Trade Balance
**Definition:** Difference between a country's exports and imports of goods.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** If it exports more than it imports, it has a trade surplus.

### Public Deficit
**Definition:** Situation in which government spending exceeds state revenues.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** If the government spends more than it collects, it incurs a deficit.

### Public Debt
**Definition:** Set of the state's financial obligations accumulated over time.  
**Source:** Bank of Spain  
**Example:** A country finances its deficit by issuing debt.

### Monetary Policy
**Definition:** Actions by the central bank to control the money supply and interest rates.  
**Source:** ECB  
**Example:** Raising interest rates to curb inflation.

### Fiscal Policy
**Definition:** Use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** Increasing public spending to stimulate the economy.

### Productivity
**Definition:** Relationship between output obtained and resources used.  
**Source:** OECD  
**Example:** More output with fewer resources implies higher productivity.

### Capital
**Definition:** Set of goods used to produce other goods and services.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** Machinery and tools in a factory.

### Labor
**Definition:** Human effort applied to the production of goods and services.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** Hours worked by employees.

### Opportunity Cost
**Definition:** Value of the best alternative not chosen.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** Choosing to study instead of working means forgoing income.

### Market
**Definition:** Place or system where buyers and sellers interact.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** The market determines prices through supply and demand.

### CPI
**Definition:** Index that measures the evolution of the price of a representative basket of goods and services.  
**Source:** INE  
**Example:** It is used to calculate inflation.

### Exchange Rate
**Definition:** Price of one currency in terms of another.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** 1 EUR = 1.1 USD indicates the exchange rate.

### Perfect Competition
**Definition:** Market structure characterized by a large number of buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, freedom of entry and exit, and perfect information.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** The market for basic agricultural products often approximates perfect competition.

### Monopoly
**Definition:** Market structure where a single producer offers a good or service with no close substitutes, controlling prices.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** A state-owned drinking water company in a city is often a natural monopoly.

### Oligopoly
**Definition:** Market structure dominated by a small number of firms that can influence prices and output.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** The airline market in many countries is an oligopoly.

### Externality
**Definition:** External effect that the activity of one economic agent has on third parties without them participating in the transaction.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** A factory that pollutes a river generates a negative externality for downstream fishermen.

### Public Good
**Definition:** Good that is non-rival (consumption by one does not reduce availability to others) and non-excludable (consumption cannot be prevented).  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** Street lighting is a typical public good.

### Income per Capita
**Definition:** Average income per person in a country, calculated by dividing total GDP by the population.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** A country with GDP of 1 trillion and 50 million inhabitants has a per capita income of 20,000.

### Lorenz Curve
**Definition:** Graphical representation showing the distribution of income or wealth in a population.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** The further the curve is from the equality diagonal, the greater the inequality.

### Gini Coefficient
**Definition:** Numerical measure of inequality in income distribution, ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (maximum inequality).  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** A Gini coefficient of 0.25 indicates low inequality; one of 0.60 indicates high inequality.

### Stagflation
**Definition:** Economic situation characterized by high inflation, high unemployment, and stagnant economic growth.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** The oil crisis of the 1970s caused stagflation in several Western economies.

### Business Cycle
**Definition:** Fluctuations in economic activity around its growth trend, with phases of expansion, peak, recession, and trough.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** The 2008 financial crisis marked the beginning of a recessionary phase of the business cycle.

### Keynesian Multiplier
**Definition:** Amplifying effect of initial spending on total output, whereby an increase in spending generates a larger increase in income.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** If the multiplier is 2, an increase of 100 million in government spending raises GDP by 200 million.

### PPP
**Definition:** Purchasing Power Parity. Method to compare purchasing power between countries by adjusting for price differences.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** GDP per capita in PPP allows comparison of real living standards between countries with different price levels.

### Risk Premium
**Definition:** Extra yield that investors demand to assume additional risk, especially in sovereign debt.  
**Source:** Bank of Spain  
**Example:** A country's risk premium measures the difference between the interest on its debt and that of an asset considered safe.

### Liquidity
**Definition:** Ability of an asset to be converted into cash quickly without significant loss of value.  
**Source:** Bank of Spain  
**Example:** Cash is the most liquid asset; real estate has low liquidity.

### Monetary Depreciation
**Definition:** Loss of value of a currency against others in a flexible exchange rate system.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** If the euro goes from 1.20 to 1.10 dollars, the euro depreciates against the dollar.

### Working Capital
**Definition:** Financial resources needed to cover a company's operating cycle, calculated as current assets minus current liabilities.  
**Source:** Finance textbook  
**Example:** A company with current assets of 500,000 and current liabilities of 300,000 has working capital of 200,000.

### Moral Hazard
**Definition:** Situation where an agent takes excessive risks because they do not bear the negative consequences, being protected by insurance or a guarantee.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** A bank that knows it will be bailed out by the government may take excessive risks in its investments.

### Adverse Selection
**Definition:** Phenomenon where lack of information leads to higher-risk agents being the most interested in purchasing insurance or participating in a market.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** In health insurance, those with the highest risk are the most likely to purchase coverage.

### Game Theory
**Definition:** Branch of economics that studies strategic decisions where each agent's outcome depends on the actions of others.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** The prisoner's dilemma is a classic example of game theory.

### Comparative Advantage
**Definition:** Ability of a country or producer to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another, the theoretical basis of international trade.  
**Source:** David Ricardo / Economics textbook  
**Example:** Even if a country is more efficient in everything, it benefits from specializing in what it does relatively best.

### Savings
**Definition:** Part of income not spent on consumption, set aside for future use or investment.  
**Source:** Bank of Spain  
**Example:** If a person earns €2,000 per month and spends €1,800, their savings are €200 per month.

### Investment
**Definition:** Use of resources to acquire capital goods that enable future production to increase.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** A company purchasing new machinery is an investment.

### Subsidy
**Definition:** Economic aid granted by the government to companies or individuals to encourage certain activities or alleviate burdens.  
**Source:** IMF  
**Example:** A public transport subsidy reduces the ticket price for users.

### Tariff
**Definition:** Tax levied on imported products, used to protect domestic industry or raise revenue.  
**Source:** WTO  
**Example:** A 10% tariff on imported cars makes their price higher compared to domestic ones.

### Quasi-Money
**Definition:** Highly liquid financial assets that are not cash but can be easily converted into money, such as savings deposits or investment funds.  
**Source:** Bank of Spain  
**Example:** Fixed-term deposits are considered quasi-money in monetary aggregates.

### Liquidity Trap
**Definition:** Situation where interest rates are so low that expansionary monetary policy loses effectiveness, as agents prefer to hold cash.  
**Source:** Keynes / ECB  
**Example:** Japan experienced a liquidity trap during the 1990s.

### Phillips Curve
**Definition:** Inverse relationship between the unemployment rate and the inflation rate in the short run.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** When unemployment falls, wages and prices tend to rise.

### Inferior Good
**Definition:** Good whose demand decreases when consumer income rises, being substituted by higher-quality goods.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** Store-brand products may be an inferior good for some consumers.

### Normal Good
**Definition:** Good whose demand increases when consumer income rises.  
**Source:** Economics textbook  
**Example:** Foreign vacations are typically a normal good: as income increases, consumption increases.

### GDP per Capita
**Definition:** Indicator that divides Gross Domestic Product by the number of inhabitants, approximating the average wealth level per person.  
**Source:** World Bank  
**Example:** A country with GDP of 500 billion and 25 million inhabitants has a GDP per capita of €20,000.
