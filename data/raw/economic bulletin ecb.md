# Economic Bulletin: Euro Area Macroeconomic Conditions, Financial Stability, and Policy Outlook (2022–2026)

## Abstract

This bulletin provides an assessment of macroeconomic conditions in the euro area from 2022 to early 2026, based on recent data and institutional projections. The analysis focuses on inflation dynamics, economic growth, financial stability, and policy responses, incorporating perspectives consistent with the European Central Bank (ECB), the International Monetary Fund (IMF), and the World Bank.

## 1. Introduction

Since 2022, the euro area economy has undergone a significant adjustment process characterized by a sharp rise in inflation, rapid monetary tightening, and evolving global conditions. Inflation increased due to a combination of factors, including energy price shocks linked to the Russia–Ukraine conflict, supply chain disruptions, and strong post-pandemic demand.

Headline inflation peaked at approximately 10.6% in October 2022, the highest level observed since the creation of the euro. In response, the ECB implemented a rapid tightening cycle, while fiscal policy gradually shifted away from expansionary pandemic measures.

By 2025, inflation had declined considerably, although core inflation remained above target and economic growth slowed.

## 2. Global Economic Environment

### 2.1 External Demand

Global growth moderated significantly after the post-pandemic rebound:

| Year | Global GDP Growth (%) |
|------|----------------------|
| 2021 | 6.0                  |
| 2022 | 3.5                  |
| 2023 | 3.0                  |
| 2024 | 3.1                  |
| 2025 | 3.2 (est.)           |

According to IMF projections, global growth remains constrained by weaker expansion in advanced economies, slower growth in China, and ongoing geopolitical fragmentation. These factors have limited external demand for euro area exports.

### 2.2 Commodity Markets

Commodity price developments have been a key driver of inflation dynamics:

- Natural gas prices (TTF benchmark) exceeded €300/MWh in 2022 before falling below €50/MWh during 2024  
- Brent crude oil prices fluctuated between $70 and $100 per barrel between 2023 and 2025  
- Food prices increased significantly in 2022, with the FAO index rising by approximately 14% year-on-year  

The normalization of energy prices contributed to disinflation, although commodity markets remain exposed to geopolitical risks.

## 3. Domestic Macroeconomic Developments

### 3.1 Inflation Dynamics

#### Headline Inflation

| Year | Inflation (%) |
|------|--------------|
| 2022 | 8.4          |
| 2023 | 5.4          |
| 2024 | 2.9          |
| 2025 | 2.4 (est.)   |
| 2026 | 2.1 (proj.)  |

The decline in inflation reflects base effects, easing energy prices, and tighter financial conditions.

#### Core Inflation

Core inflation has been more persistent:

- Around 3.5% in 2024  
- Moderating to approximately 3.0–3.2% in 2025  
- Expected to gradually converge toward target levels in 2026  

Persistence is mainly explained by wage growth and services sector dynamics.

#### Services vs. Goods Inflation

- Goods inflation declined rapidly due to normalization in supply chains  
- Services inflation remained elevated, around 3.5–4.0%, reflecting labor cost pressures  

### 3.2 Economic Growth

#### GDP Performance

| Year | GDP Growth (%) |
|------|----------------|
| 2022 | 3.4            |
| 2023 | 0.5            |
| 2024 | 0.8            |
| 2025 | 1.1–1.3 (est.) |
| 2026 | 1.3–1.5 (proj.)|

Economic growth slowed significantly due to restrictive monetary policy and weak external demand.

#### Demand Components

Private consumption remained supported by employment growth but was constrained by reduced real incomes in 2022–2023, with gradual recovery thereafter.

Investment declined, particularly in interest-sensitive sectors such as construction, as higher financing costs and uncertainty weighed on business activity.

Government spending gradually normalized following pandemic-related expansion, although targeted measures remained in place.

### 3.3 Labor Market

Labor markets remained resilient:

- Unemployment rate around 6.4–6.6% in 2025  
- Employment levels at historical highs in several member states  

Nominal wage growth reached approximately 4.5–5.0%, with real wages gradually recovering after previous declines. Wage developments continue to contribute to underlying inflation pressures.

## 4. Monetary Policy Developments

### 4.1 Policy Rate Evolution

The ECB increased its key policy rates rapidly:

- Deposit facility rate: from -0.50% in 2021 to 4.00% in 2023  
- Rates remained at restrictive levels through 2025  

Market expectations point to a gradual easing cycle beginning in 2026, depending on inflation developments.

### 4.2 Balance Sheet Policies

- Net asset purchases under APP and PEPP were discontinued  
- Balance sheet reduction proceeded gradually  
- Liquidity conditions tightened as securities matured  

### 4.3 Transmission Channels

Higher interest rates increased borrowing costs for households and firms, leading to reduced mortgage demand and lower investment.

Bank lending standards tightened significantly, according to ECB surveys, resulting in slower credit growth.

Inflation expectations stabilized close to the ECB’s target, supporting policy credibility.

## 5. Financial Sector and Stability

### 5.1 Banking Sector

The banking sector remained resilient:

- CET1 ratios around 14–15%  
- Profitability improved, with return on equity between 8% and 10%  
- Non-performing loans remained low, around 2–2.5%  

### 5.2 Credit Conditions

Financing conditions tightened across sectors, with reduced loan demand and higher borrowing costs, particularly affecting small and medium-sized enterprises.

### 5.3 Asset Markets

Housing markets showed signs of correction in several countries, with declining prices and reduced transaction volumes.

Equity markets experienced increased volatility and valuation adjustments due to higher discount rates.

### 5.4 Financial Stability Risks

Key risks include exposure to the real estate sector, increased debt servicing burdens, and vulnerabilities in highly leveraged firms.

## 6. Fiscal Policy Developments

### 6.1 Fiscal Stance

Fiscal policy shifted from expansionary to broadly neutral, with the gradual withdrawal of energy support measures.

### 6.2 Public Debt

Public debt remained elevated, with debt-to-GDP ratios around 88–92%, while interest costs increased due to higher rates.

### 6.3 Policy Challenges

Authorities face the challenge of balancing fiscal consolidation with growth support, alongside addressing long-term sustainability and demographic pressures.

## 7. Structural Developments

### 7.1 Energy Transition

Investment in renewable energy increased significantly, with renewables accounting for over 40% of electricity generation by 2025. The transition continues to involve short-term cost pressures.

### 7.2 Digitalization

Digital adoption accelerated across sectors, including the integration of artificial intelligence technologies, although productivity gains remain uneven.

### 7.3 Labor Market Structural Issues

Structural challenges include skill mismatches, an aging workforce, and regional disparities in employment.

## 8. Outlook and Projections

### 8.1 Baseline Scenario

- Inflation expected to converge toward 2.0–2.2% by 2026  
- Core inflation gradually declining toward target  
- GDP growth projected at 1.3–1.5%  
- Gradual monetary easing anticipated starting in 2026  

### 8.2 Alternative Scenarios

In an upside scenario, faster disinflation and stronger global growth could support earlier policy easing.

In a downside scenario, persistent inflation and weak external demand could prolong restrictive monetary conditions.

## 9. Risk Assessment

Inflation risks remain linked to wage pressures and energy price volatility.

Growth risks are associated with tight credit conditions, weak investment, and external shocks.

Financial risks include potential asset price corrections and corporate refinancing challenges.

## 10. Policy Implications

Monetary policy is expected to remain restrictive until inflation is firmly anchored at target, followed by gradual and data-dependent easing.

Fiscal policy should prioritize targeted support measures while maintaining sustainability.

Structural reforms remain essential to enhance productivity, improve labor market efficiency, and support the green and digital transitions.


The euro area economy has made significant progress in reducing inflation since 2022, supported by monetary tightening and the normalization of energy prices. However, underlying inflationary pressures persist, particularly in the services sector, and economic growth remains moderate.

The outlook suggests gradual convergence toward price stability alongside subdued growth, with policy coordination playing a central role in maintaining macroeconomic stability.
