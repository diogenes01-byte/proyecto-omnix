This bulletin provides a comprehensive assessment of macroeconomic conditions in the euro area between 2022 and early 2026, with a focus on inflation dynamics, economic growth, financial stability, and policy responses. ## 1. Introduction

The euro area economy has undergone a period of significant adjustment since 2022, marked by an inflation surge, rapid monetary tightening, and evolving global economic conditions. The combination of supply-side shocks, including energy price volatility and supply chain disruptions, alongside strong post-pandemic demand, resulted in the highest inflation rates observed in decades.

In response, monetary authorities implemented a historically rapid tightening cycle, while fiscal policy gradually shifted from expansionary to more neutral stances. By 2025, inflation had declined substantially, but underlying pressures remained, and economic growth slowed.

This bulletin evaluates these developments in detail, with particular attention to transmission mechanisms and structural challenges.

## 2. Global Economic Environment

### 2.1 External Demand

Global growth slowed from approximately 6.0% in 2021 to around 3.0% in 2023 and stabilized near 3.1% in 2024–2025.

Key external developments:

- Weak demand from major trading partners  
- Slower growth in emerging markets  
- Trade fragmentation and geopolitical tensions  

These factors constrained euro area export performance.

---

### 2.2 Commodity Markets

Energy markets played a central role:

- Natural gas prices surged in 2022 before declining sharply in 2023  
- Oil prices remained volatile, fluctuating between $70 and $100 per barrel  
- Food prices increased significantly due to supply disruptions  

Commodity price normalization contributed to disinflation but remained a source of risk.

- Peak: ~10.6% (2022)

---

#### Core Inflation

Core inflation proved more persistent:

- Averaged ~3.5% in 2024  
- Moderated to ~3.1% in 2025  

Persistence driven by:

- Wage growth  
- Services sector dynamics  
- Gradual pass-through of past shocks  

---

#### Services vs. Goods Inflation

- Goods inflation declined rapidly due to easing supply chains  
- Services inflation remained elevated (~3.5–4.0%)  

This divergence highlights structural differences in price formation.
